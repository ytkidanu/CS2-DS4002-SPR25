Image Recognition Technology and its Role in Agriculture

Lynn Anderson

·

Follow

5 min read
·
May 23, 2023

As the human population grows, more pressure is being placed on the agricultural industry to maximize efficiency. It is becoming more and more important for farmers to optimize growing conditions for their specific crop in order to increase yields. It can be difficult for farmers to pinpoint exactly how to best prepare the soil, tend to the plants, and when to harvest. Climate related factors such as rainfall, temperature, and humidity have a huge influence on whether or not a crop succeeds, and each crop is unique in the nutrients it requires, diseases it is susceptible to, and conditions it grows best in. With the help of technology to inform and guide farmers in their crop production and management, yields can be optimized more so than by just human judgement alone. The more the agricultural industry can build on current knowledge and methods to predict weather forecasts and optimize soil health, the better we can accommodate the growing population without having to destroy additional natural ecosystems.

The latest technologies for gaining understanding to how best to care for crops are utilizing machine learning and satellite imagery, often in combination. There are a multitude of examples — robots being developed to perform time-consuming tasks- like monitoring and harvesting crops- and smart sensors used for their ability to accurately detect diseases, pests, contaminants, weeds, and chemicals in the growing environments. Additionally, the use of drone and satellite imagery to monitor growth, and the use of machine learning algorithms to diagnose problems and analyze soil components has become more important in recent years.

One field of machine learning already in widespread use throughout the agricultural industry is that of image recognition technology. Image recognition technology is a branch of machine learning where models learn to distinguish important features and classify images. It is a sub-category of computer vision technology that deals with recognizing features in images by interpreting the patterns in the pixels. This technology is based on deep learning, or the use of multiple layers of neural networks in the development of the image recognition algorithm. The algorithms are trained on large datasets of images to learn the patterns and characteristics of different objects. The trained model is then used to accurately classify new images into different categories. Image recognition can be very helpful for the detection of plant diseases and pests, and for general monitoring of overall crop and soil health.

The process of developing an image recognition algorithm typically requires multiple steps. To begin, a large dataset consisting of labeled images are preprocessed to remove noise, distortions, or anything else that could hinder the learning process. This step can involve resizing, cropping, or adjusting the contrast and brightness of the images. Next, these images- which have already been identified and labeled by a human- are then used to train the algorithm to recognize patterns and features in different types of images. The algorithm is taught to identify and isolate relevant parts of the image that will enable it to distinguish between different objects or categories. Once the algorithm can recognize and extract important features, it is trained on the labeled dataset of images. After that initial training, the algorithm is tested on a separate dataset of images to evaluate its accuracy and performance. This last step is crucial to identifying any errors or weaknesses in the model. Finally, once the model has been tested and verified, it can be used to accurately classify new images into different categories.

In agriculture, image recognition technology algorithms are being used for identifying unhealthy crops or symptoms of nutrient deficiencies, on both a close up and larger scale. Some implementations include a mobile application that recognizes diseases or symptoms, and aerial imaging analysis that detects issues from a larger perspective. I will go over two specific examples- however this is just a brief glimpse of what is out there.

Peat, a German tech start-up, developed an application in 2015 based on image recognition technology. The app, called Plantix, can identify underlying nutrient deficiencies in soil, plant pests, or diseases that may be responsible for a given issue. The mobile app is relatively simple to use- the farmer takes images of their plants using a smartphone, and the app then identifies the problem. Once the problem has been diagnosed, the app will direct farmers as which methods or fertilizers will help to prevent diseases or deficiencies responsible for the symptoms. It also makes broader suggestions regarding what techniques may be beneficial to improve soil health and increase harvest quality and yields, based on both crop performance and considering the location and local climate. The app also contains an extensive database of images of diseases, conditions, and visible symptoms farmers can look through.

Another example of image recognition technology supporting agricultural practices is through the analysis of aerial images. VineView, based out of Canada, has developed drone-based aerial imaging methods for monitoring crop health, specifically for wine grapes. Aerial-based sensors operating under a variety of wavelengths in combination with advanced algorithms help grape growers make informed decisions regarding their vineyard management. With insight from the data, grape farmers can optimize irrigation strategies, harvest planning, and disease mapping. For the aerial imaging, the drone captures data from fields, and then data is transferred to a computer. The captured images are then analyzed by the algorithm to provide a detailed report on the current health and status of the crop. This technology can analyze and track crop health, make yield predictions, and detect malnutrition much faster than a human walking through a field. It also helps the farmer to identify pests and diseases from a larger scope, helping guide the timely use of pest control and fertilizer use.

These are just a couple examples- other AI technologies have been developed to decode soil composition by identifying the soil microbes and nutrients present in a sample. Broader uses of soil biology, such as remediation of contaminated soils, detecting pathogens that are harmful for human health, and developing soil-based carbon sequestration, are also in use. Additionally, using improved weather predictions and climate modeling will help suggest what crops might be best in a given season for a specific location. As machine learning models become more accurate and encompassing with regards to agriculture, farmers will be able to make increasingly better decisions and grow crops more efficiently.
